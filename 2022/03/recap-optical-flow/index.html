Recap: Optical Flow

March 19, 2022 in Algorithm, Computer Vision

What is Optical Flow? To introduce it briefly, it could be explained as the motion of objects between consecutive frames of sequence. The objects‚Äô motion are projected form 3-D space to a 2-D image plane. This algorithm’s derivation is caused by the relative movement between the object and camera.

There are many applications have something to do with optical flow, like Structure from Motion , Object Tracking, Video Compression and Video Stabilization.

To derivate and implement optical flow, there are some constraints for considerations:

  1. The pixel intensities of objects are identical between consecutive frames.
  2. Time between adjacent frames is continuous or the motion of objects changes slightly (smooth).
  3. The neighbors of target pixel have the resemble movements.

I used to have some research in this field so it’s time for some organizations and recaptions.

General Representation

We discuss optical flow begin with two frames relation like figure below shows.

We can represent the image into equations:

$$I(x, y, t) = I(x+dx, y+dy, t+dt)$$

where I is image intensity, x is horizontal coordinate, y is vertical coordinate, t is frame timestamp.

Therefore, we can use the Taylor Series Approximation to expand the polynomial. It will become:

$$I(x+dx, y+dy, t+dt) \approx I(x, y, t) + \frac{\partial I}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}dy + \frac{\partial I}{\partial t}dt $$

Since we have defined constraint:

  • The pixel intensities of objects are identical between consecutive frames.

thus,

$$ \frac{\partial I}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}dy + \frac{\partial I}{\partial t}dt = 0$$

It can be divided by dt to derive the optical flow equation.

$$ \frac{\partial I}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} + \frac{\partial I}{\partial t} = 0$$

We can futhermore express the equation using matrix form:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} I_x & I_y \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = -I_t $$

$$ \begin{cases} \text{$ I_x=\frac{\partial I}{\partial x},\ \rm{grident\ along\ x} $} \\ \text{$ I_y=\frac{\partial I}{\partial y},\ \rm{grident\ along\ y} $} \\ \text{$ I_t=\frac{\partial I}{\partial t},\ \rm{grident\ along\ t} $} \\ \text{$ u=\frac{dx}{dt},\ \rm{instaneous\ speed\ in\ x\ direction} $} \\ \text{$ v=\frac{dy}{dt},\ \rm{instaneous\ speed\ in\ y\ direction} $} \end{cases} $$

This is the general representation of optical flow in form of matrix. For the target pixel, solve u=dx/dt and v=dy/dt to determine movement over time.

Cause we have two unknown variables u and v with only one formula, this aperture problem needs to be solved by implementing some methods. Different methods including:

  • Gradient based
  • Block-matching based
  • Energy based
  • Deep-Learning based

Depending on different ways implemented, we are going to introduce two classic methods, the Sparse Optical Flow and Dense Optical Flow. There are articles exhibit the results using GIF of these two methods which are very clear and straightful.

Sparse Optical Flow: Lucas-Kanade

Sparse optical flow selects a sparse feature set of pixels to track its velocity vectors, that is, motion. Interesting features such as edges and corners are passed in the optical flow function through frames to ensure that the same points are being tracked.

To extract those features, sometimes we need corner detector like Harris or Shi-Tomasi . These detectors calculating the gradients of intensity according to each target point and classify the wanted features.

As one of the representatives, Lucas-Kanade method estimate the motion of interesting features(corners) by comparing two consecutive frames. It uses a small nxn window (neighborhood) around the features detected by corner detector for solving the optical flow formula and takes the constraints we have mentioned before as assumption.

Each pixel in the window has its own optical flow equation, express into matrix formula:

$$\begin{bmatrix} I_x(q_1) & I_y(q_1) \\ I_x(q_2) & I_y(q_2) \\ … & … \\ I_x(q_i) & I_y(q_i) \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -I_t(q_1) \\ -I_t(q_2) \\ … \\ -I_t(q_i) \end{bmatrix} $$

This over-determined equation can be solved by pseudo-inverse .

$$Ax = b,\ x = (A^TA)^{-1}A^Tb$$

Identified features are tracked and optical flow vectors of these points are iteratively computed.

Something important is that Lucas-Kanade method only works for small movements (from our assumption) and fails when there is large motion. OpenCV adopts pyramids for implementing Lucas-Kanade. It computes optical flow along with scales, thus in a high-level view, small motions are neglected as we escalates the pyramid and large motions are reduced to small motions.

Dense Optical Flow: Farneback

Dense optical flow attempts to compute the optical flow vector for every pixel of each frame. In comparison with sparse optical flow, dense optical flow has slower computation but more accurate. It is more suitable for applications such as learning structure from motion and video segmentation.

First, the method approximates the windows of image frames by quadratic polynomials through polynomial expansion transform.

Second, by observing how the polynomial transforms under translation (motion), a method doing displacement estimate from polynomial expansion coefficients is defined.

Quadratic polynomials represent the local coordinate system which approximate some neighborhood of each pixel.

$$f(x, y) \approx r_1 + r_2x + r_3y + r_4x^2 + r_5y^2 + r_6xy$$ $$ = \begin{bmatrix} x & y \end{bmatrix}^T\begin{bmatrix} r_4 & r_6/2 \\ r_6/2 & r_5 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} r_2 \\ r_3 \end{bmatrix}^T\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} + r_1 $$ $$ = X^TAX + b^TX + c$$

where A is a symmetric matrix, b is a vector and c is a scaler.

If a polynomial undergoes an ideal translation:

$$ f_1(x) = X^TA_1X + b_1^TX + c_1 $$

and move a global displacement by d:

$$ f_2(x) = f_1(X - d) = (X - d)^TA_1(X - d) + b_1^T(X - d) + c_1 $$ $$ = X^TA_1X + (b_1 - 2A_1d)^TX + d^TA_1d - b_1^Td + c_1 $$ $$ = X^TA_2X + b_2^TX + c_2 $$

where

$$ A_2=A_1 $$ $$ b_2=b_1 - 2A_1d $$ $$ c_2=d^TA_1d ‚àí b_1^Tùëë + c_1 $$

$$ d = -\frac{1}{2}A_1^{‚àí1}(b_2 ‚àí b_1)\ when\ A_1\ is\ nonsingular $$

Practical consideration in signal (pixels in one window) transition, using single polynomial

$$ X^TAX + b^TX + c $$

to represent entire signal, and a global translation d relating the two signals, are quite unrealistic.

$$ A(X) = \frac{A_1(X) + A_2(X)}{2} $$

Also introduce

$$ \Delta b(X) = ‚àí\frac{1}{2}(b_2(X) ‚àí b_1(ùëã)) $$

to obtain the primary constraint

$$ A(X)d(X) = \Delta b(X) $$

Solve

$$ d =(A^TA)^{‚àí1} (A^T \Delta b) $$

pointwise may be too noisy.

Use constraint as assumption, we can integrate information over a neighborhood of each pixel.

$$ e(X) = \sum_{\Delta X \in I} \omega (\Delta X) ||A(X + \Delta X)d(X) ‚àí \Delta b(X + \Delta X)||^2 $$ $$ d(X) = (\sum \omega A^TA)^{‚àí1} (\sum \omega A)^T \Delta b $$ $$ \omega (\Delta X):\ weight\ function\ for\ the\ points\ in\ the\ neighborhood $$

The coefficients are estimated from a weighted least squares fit to the signal values in the neighborhood, which also known as normalized convolution.
